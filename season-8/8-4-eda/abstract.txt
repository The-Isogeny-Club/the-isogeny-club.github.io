<p>We investigate the security assumptions underlying isogeny-based cryptography, focusing on principally polarized superspecial abelian surfaces over finite fields. Our main tool is the refined Humbert invariant, an arithmetic object attached to genus-2 curves. We show that computing this invariant is as hard as solving the computational isogeny problem, giving a new geometric perspective on central cryptographic questions. We then apply this framework to problems such as isogeny-degree distributions of elliptic curves and the splitting behavior of isogeny graphs of superspecial abelian surfaces.</p>
